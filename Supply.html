Section 01 · The opening

Why is your supply chain breaking?

Economics 101 promises balance: supply meets demand, the scale sits level, the shelves stay full. The textbook version is beautiful. It's also a lie — or at least, it's only true on average. The moment you watch it in motion, the myth starts to crack.

Fig. 01 — Supply & demand, in motion Equilibrium seeking…
TAKEAWAY

Balance is the myth. The textbook draws two smooth curves crossing at a single point. Real supply and demand are noisy, late, and uncorrelated — and it's the noise, not the average, that breaks the chain.

Three Vendors, three promises, three realities.

Every Vendor is headed for a 120-day delivery. But the band around that number — the error bar — is what determines whether your facility runs or your facility stops. One of these Vendors is a coin flip. One is a clockwork.

03 · C — The commute you already know

What Coefficient of Variation means in English.

You don't need a degree in statistics to feel this. You just need to have driven in a few different cities. Every one of these commutes averages the same 30 minutes door-to-door. But nobody who lives in them would ever call them equivalent.

THE MATH, IN ONE LINE

Coefficient of Variation — the number that actually matters — is just the standard deviation divided by the mean. CoV = σ ÷ μ. It measures how noisy a process is relative to its average. A 10-minute swing matters a lot for a 30-minute commute. It barely registers for a transatlantic flight.

THE PAYOFF

All three commuters averaged the same 30 minutes last year. But the LA commuter left home 40 minutes earlier than the Indy one — every day, just to hit the same 9am meeting. That's safety stock. That's what variability costs you.

TAKEAWAY

Same 120-day average, wildly different risk. Vendor A's band is six times wider than Vendor C's. The bid sheet collapsed that into a single number — and charged you $2.00 less per unit for the privilege of not knowing.

THE REAL BILL

Vendor A saved you $2 per unit on the bid sheet — $79,820 / year in promised savings. It also cost you 4,404 units you couldn't ship to customers. At any realistic gross margin, one of those numbers is much larger than the other. And that's before anyone asks about the phone calls.

TAKEAWAY

Variability in delivery becomes variability in inventory. Vendor A's 9 stockout weeks weren't a vendor problem — they were a planning problem. The contract was identical. The schedule of arrivals was not. And your warehouse felt every missed week.

THE LESSON

A lot of leaders try to chase one number — I catch myself doing it all the time. Average delivery time is 120 days. But that's not the story of this supply chain. It's the unpredictability around that 120 days — the variability in supply. Consistency is just as important as speed. The tortoise vs. the rabbit.

TAKEAWAY

Pick the vendor whose variability fits inside yours. You can't control demand — customers will be customers. You can control the variability you invite in on the supply side. The cheapest bid is rarely the cheapest decision. The most predictable one usually is.
