Takeaways

Eight stages, three products, one journey from the earth to a kitchen drawer. What follows is less a summary and more a landscape — a map of where competition, capital, and risk live across the supply chain. Some stages are walls. Some are battlefields. Some are forgotten. None are the same.

A note on what this is. Everything that follows is a generalization of my own thinking about how the global supply chain is structured — informed by research, but ultimately one person's reading of an enormous, messy, and constantly shifting system. Read it as a perspective, not a verdict.

The map

Stage landscape

Each stage plotted on two axes: Investment Barrier (how hard it is to enter — capital, regulation, expertise) and Number of Players (how concentrated or competitive the stage is). The four quadrants reveal four kinds of business.

Top-left
Battlefields

Top-right
Elastic

The stages

Eight reflections

A reading of each stage in its own right — what kind of business it is, what its quirks are, where I'd look if I were building, investing, or just trying to understand where things break.

01 Earth
The Earth
The Wall
The most delicate stage of the supply chain — and the most rewarding if your due diligence is right.
  • Location, location, location. Surveying, testing, measuring, weather, climate, accessibility — the work that decides whether anything below it is even possible.
  • High government regulation and permits. Red tape is the moat. Mining licenses, water rights, zoning, environmental review — the friction is the protection.
  • Long horizons. Minimum 5–7 years to ROI; 20–30 years is normal before real profits show. Long-term planning and patient capital are the price of entry.
  • Few producers, sticky business. When the work is done correctly, the position is essentially permanent. The land doesn't move.
  • Building block of everything downstream. The most fragile part of the chain — and the one most worth doing well.
02 Sorting
Sorting
Overlooked
The forgotten part of the supply chain — and probably the one with the most innovation upside.
  • The largest source of waste in the entire chain. What gets sorted out is what gets thrown away. Volume of waste is enormous.
  • Innovation upside hides here. Anyone who can turn that waste into a profitable product is unlocking value almost no one is competing for.
  • Almost always co-located. Sorting happens at or near the point of extraction or harvest — moving unsorted material is too expensive.
  • An unglamorous stage that gets minimal attention from investors, founders, and operators alike.
03 Refinement
Refinement
Elastic
Where the product genuinely becomes usable — and where the chain starts to travel.
  • Geographic separation begins here. Refinement typically happens further from extraction or harvest than sorting did — material can finally travel.
  • The cleaning step. What enters the refinement stage is rough; what leaves it is the working substance — peel from flesh, bark from wood, oxygen from iron.
  • Sophisticated technologies and processes. Big science and big chemistry live here.
  • Streamlined and concentrated. Larger factories, fewer of them, optimized to throughput.
04 Transformation
Transformation
The Wall
The largest energy and chemical use step in the chain — and the second most delicate, in my view. It could use more competition.
  • Highest energy and chemical intensity. The blast furnaces, the kraft digesters, the pasteurizers. This is where the chain's footprint actually lives.
  • The product begins to take final shape. Pig iron becomes steel; pulp becomes paper; sauce gets its texture.
  • Dirty and rigid. The processes don't bend easily; capital is sunk; output specs are narrow.
  • Hard to start, hard to compete in. Large initial investment, "not as sexy" as consumer-facing stages, fewer entrants.
  • The second most delicate stage, after Earth — and the one most starved for new competition.
05 Finishing
Finishing
Elastic
The icing on the cake — where a small number of inputs gets turned into a wide variety of outputs.
  • Few inputs, many outputs. Finishers transform a handful of standardized intermediates into countless final products. The variety lives here.
  • Batch manufacturing dominates. Most finishers run batches rather than continuous lines — flexibility is the value.
  • Planning is everything. Demand and supply forecasting must stay in tight sync; mistakes ripple instantly.
  • Manual labor returns. A lot of the work is hands-on, and a lot of it has been outsourced to the developing world.
06 Assembly
Assembly
Elastic
The funnel point of the supply chain — many inputs converge into a few finished outputs, and any one of them can hold the line up.
  • Many inputs, few outputs. The opposite of finishing — lots of components flow in, one product comes out.
  • Delays compound. If any component upstream is missing, the entire assembly stops. Most schedule slips originate not in assembly itself but in its inputs.
  • Hard to fully automate. Manual dexterity, frequent product spec changes, and tight tolerances mean humans still handle a lot of it.
  • Concentrated in the developing world. Labor-cost arbitrage drives the geography.
07 Distribution
Distribution
The Wall
Almost impossible to break into — and where most of the chain's corruption and political leverage live.
  • The capital costs are brutal. Boats, trains, trucks, warehouses, terminals. The infrastructure required is on a different order of magnitude than any other stage.
  • The most corruption-prone stage. In both developed and developing markets — the historical American railroad era, and "papers and papers" mindsets in third-world ports.
  • Where unions form. High concentration of labor at fixed nodes (ports, rail yards) makes labor organization both natural and powerful.
  • Geopolitical chokepoints decide everything. The Strait of Hormuz, Suez, Panama, Malacca — narrow geographies hold disproportionate global power.
  • Heavy government oversight. Where states most actively shape commerce.
08 Consumption
Consumption
Battlefield
The most competitive stage — and, I think, the most risky business segment in the entire chain.
  • Closest to the consumer, most competitive. Margins are thin, brands are easy to start, and someone is always doing it cheaper.
  • Delivery has fundamentally shifted. The last 20 years have moved enormous volume from "consumer fetches it" to "delivered to the door" — a structural change still in progress.
  • The largest signal-to-noise gap in the chain. Producers are far from customers in volume and variety; the disconnect makes demand forecasting genuinely hard.
  • Small perceptions move large markets. A trend, a viral moment, a new packaging shape can swing demand wildly with little warning.
  • The most risky business segment in the entire supply chain — and the one where most consumer attention lives.

Every product in your home is the end of a chain that someone, somewhere, decided to stop competing in. Walking the eight stages backward from a pair of scissors or a jar of applesauce isn't just a tour — it's a map of where capital, risk, and opportunity actually live.
