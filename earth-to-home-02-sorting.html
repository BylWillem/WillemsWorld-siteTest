Sorting

Sorting almost always happens at the place of origin — at the orchard, the mill yard, the mine head — because hauling unsorted material is hauling waste. Every supply chain narrows here, deciding which fragments deserve the cost of the next step.

Variety

Quality scan

Optical cameras look at every apple. Surface defects route each one to a different destination — fewer blemishes, higher value.

Lumber Sorting
▲
From forest landing to corrugate mill
01
Wood type

Corrugated board is mostly softwood — long fibers from pine and spruce make stronger, springier paper. Hardwoods contribute shorter fibers and are blended in for smoother surfaces. Hover each tree to see why.

Hover a tree to see how its fiber is used.
02
Diameter

Logs are sorted by butt-end diameter at the mill yard. Big logs are too valuable for pulping — they go to the sawmill for boards. Pulpwood is the medium-and-smaller class.

Hover a log to see where it goes.
03
Log grading

Even within pulpwood, logs are graded by straightness, knots, and rot. Pulping is forgiving — but only up to a point.

Hover a log to see its grade and destination.
Iron Ore Sorting
◆
From rock at the mine head to concentrate ready for the furnace
01
Raw ore

Iron ore comes out of the ground as mixed rock. The valuable iron-bearing minerals are veined through plain stone (called gangue). The first job is to figure out how to get one out from the other.

Hover the pit or the rock to learn more.
02
Crushing

The first physical step: break the rock smaller. Crushing doesn't separate anything — it just exposes more iron veins so the next steps can work. Each piece still contains both ore and gangue.

Hover any panel to see what's happening at that step.
03
Magnetic separation

The crushed material passes a powerful magnet. Iron-rich pieces are pulled aside; non-magnetic gangue continues straight through to the tailings.

Hover either output stream to see where it goes.
04
Flotation

Final purification. Crushed ore is mixed with water and chemicals in a tank, and air bubbles are pumped in. Silica latches onto the bubbles and floats to the top as froth; the iron-rich particles sink to the bottom as concentrate.

Hover the tank to see what's happening inside.
Final output
~65% iron concentrate
From 1,000 tonnes of run-of-mine rock at ~30% iron, the sorting plant produces roughly 460 tonnes of pellet-grade concentrate. The rest — the tailings and the silica froth — stays at the mine site.
