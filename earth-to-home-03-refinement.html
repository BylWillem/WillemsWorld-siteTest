Stage Three of Eight

Refinement

Sorting decided what was worth keeping. Refinement strips away everything that isn't the raw material itself — peel from flesh, bark from wood, oxygen from iron. Where this happens matters: apples stay close to the orchard, logs travel to a mill, ore travels the world.

Apple Refinement
○
Whole apple → washed, peeled, cored flesh
Where Local processing plant, usually within the same growing region. Apples are perishable, so refinement stays close to the orchard — a Washington apple is washed and peeled in Washington, not shipped raw across the country.
Hover any panel to see what's happening at that step.
Output
Cored apple flesh
Roughly 75% of the original apple weight survives — the rest is peel, core, and seeds. Most of those byproducts go to animal feed, pectin extraction, or compost.
Lumber Refinement
▲
Pulpwood log → debarked → wood chips
Where The pulp mill, not the forest. Logs are trucked from the cut block to a fixed mill site — typically 50–200 miles away. A single mill draws from dozens of forests within its trucking radius.
Hover either panel to see what's happening at that step.
Output
Pulpable wood chips
About 90% of the debarked log becomes usable chips. Bark and sawdust are burned on-site as boiler fuel — the mill mostly powers itself with its own waste.
Iron Refinement
◆
Iron ore concentrate → blast furnace → pig iron
Where The steel mill, often on a different continent from the mine. Australian ore is smelted in China; Brazilian ore in Europe and Asia. Concentrate travels by rail to a coastal port, then by bulk freighter — sometimes 10,000+ miles from the pit before it's smelted.
Hover either panel to see what's happening at that step.
Output
~94% pure pig iron
The most violent transformation in the supply chain. Solid rock enters the top of the furnace; molten metal flows out the bottom 6–8 hours later. The iron is now usable — but still too brittle to be steel.
